Who I am

If you look at my educational background, you'll see I've studies aerospace and mechanical engineering during my undergraduate and graduate studies. Yet my research falls mainly within the computer science domain. I think it's an indication that my interests cover a wide range of topics in science and engineering. Based on my educational background, some people might call me an aerodynamicist, or a simulation engineer. On the other hand, my resarch experience tags me as a machine learning engineer, or a data scientist in general. I prefer the latter, as it gives me more flexibility to take a sneak peek of other scientific areas, such as neuroscience. I guess my curiosity would never end, and my appetite to learn more and more about a wide range of topics only grows stronger as the time goes by. And luckily, it is absolutely possible for us humans to keep learning about stuff; there is no limit on the amount of knowledge one could acquire, at least not in theory. I've found machine learning an amazing tool to discover the laws of the world we're living in, and that's what pushes me forward and makes me start my day motivated.

Past

My plan to develop solid mathematical and programming skills was initiated back in high school, when I got accepted into the National Organization for Development of Exceptional Talents. After that, I attended Aerospace Engineering program at Amirkabir University of technology, where I finished my bachelor's studies as the top of the class of 2019. Besides my studies, I was an undergraduate research assistant working on optimization and numerical simulations for transport phenomena. My undergraduate thesis was on an inverse heat transfer problem, where the goal was to estimate a boundary condition using output data from sensor. More on this project is available under research section.

Present

Currently, I’m a master of applied science student at the University of British Columbia (UBC), and a graduate research assistant at the Advanced Numerical Simulation Laboratory (ANSLab). My current research is primarily focused on the implementation of data-driven and machine learning techniques to understand and analyze the behavior of numerical methods in Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). During my research assistantship, I have implemented a modal decomposition on aerodynamic data sets, based on a technique called Principal Component Analysis (PCA). Also, on top of my PCA project, I have developed two machine learning models to detect missing flow patterns in CFD simulations. These projects are briefly introduced under research section.

Future

Overall, I’m absolutely passionate about Artificial Intelligence (AI) and what it can bring us in the future. I believe with correct (and safe!) development of intelligent models using machine learning, we’ll be able to design mind blowing engineering systems that ultimately lead to a world as depicted in science fiction movies! And that's what I see myself doing in the near future.
